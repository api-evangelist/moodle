<div></div>  <div class="acf-block flexi-block-block" id="flexi-block-block_16c2cd88d89c8820095c869f6013843c">
    <div class=" flexi-block">        <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">If you&#8217;ve been to an EdTech conference lately, you already know: you can&#8217;t grab a lukewarm coffee without bumping into a conversation about AI. They’re everywhere — and for good reason. But at a recent higher education conference, one exchange cut through the noise in a way I haven&#8217;t been able to stop thinking about.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">An attendee rushing off to a session asked me a question I wasn&#8217;t expecting: </span></p>
</div>
  <div class="quote">
    <div class="text">Is it possible to&#8230; not run an LMS in a web browser anymore? Because students are using AI agents to do their work and we have absolutely no way of knowing the difference.</div>
          <div class="name">Worried Instructional Designer</div>
      </div>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">She wasn&#8217;t asking because she wants to go back to paper. She was asking because she&#8217;s afraid — afraid that the web browser has become a door that AI agents can walk right through, invisibly, undetected.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the swarm of the conference, I never got a chance to fully respond. But if we had more time, here&#8217;s what I would have told her: </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;"><strong>The idea that agents are invisible in your LMS isn&#8217;t actually true.</strong> Not with <a href="https://moodle.com/products/lms/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Moodle LMS</a>, anyway.</span></p>
</div>
</div>
  </div>


  <div class="acf-block flexi-block-block" id="flexi-block-block_2ebc9c780e01a188cb0f38844d383350">
    <div class=" flexi-block">        <h2 id="block-h2-whats-changed--and-whats-possible">What&#8217;s changed — and what&#8217;s possible</h2>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Generative AI isn&#8217;t new. Most people have already used tools that synthesise content, draft text, or explain concepts. What&#8217;s changed is that AI can now </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">act</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">AI agents can move through systems, complete tasks, and follow multi-step instructions. In a learning environment, that means they can do many of the things we&#8217;ve relied on as signals of learner participation — submitting work, completing activities, progressing through a course. And for a long time, the assumption was that this agentic activity was simply undetectable.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">That assumption is wrong.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Joseph Thibault, founder of </span><a href="https://moodle.com/certified-integrations/cursive/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Cursive</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a Moodle <a href="https://moodle.com/certified-integrations/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Certified Integration</a>, has been building tools in the Moodle ecosystem for years — starting with writing analytics and academic integrity, and more recently turning his attention to the question of agents. His finding is direct:</span></p>
</div>
  <div class="quote">
    <div class="text">It is not impossible to detect an AI agent in your LMS. It is just a matter of using analytics in a smarter way.</div>
          <div class="name">Joseph Thibault</div>
          <div class="source">Founder, Cursive</div>
      </div>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The key is looking beyond what standard LMS logs capture. Agents and humans interact with a platform very differently. The output might look the same. The behaviour underneath usually doesn&#8217;t — and that difference is only visible if your platform is built to look for it. </span></p>
</div>
</div>
  </div>


  <div class="acf-block flexi-block-block" id="flexi-block-block_a2c5f0d7e0eb626f171f3a18078f8f42">
    <div class=" flexi-block">        <h2 id="block-h2-what-moodles-open-architecture-makes-possible">What Moodle&#8217;s open architecture makes possible</h2>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This is where Moodle&#8217;s design philosophy becomes practically important.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Moodle LMS is built to be extended. Our open framework for <a href="https://moodle.com/solutions/moodle-and-ai/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">AI in Moodle solutions</a> gives institutions full control — choice of provider, educator-level permissions, data sovereignty, and the freedom to innovate without vendor lock-in. This openness, made possible by our AI Substem, is what allows the community to respond to emerging challenges quickly, in ways that fit each institution&#8217;s own context.</span></p>
</div>
  <div class="quote">
    <div class="text">The advantage isn&#8217;t having one answer built in. It&#8217;s having a system that can respond as the questions change.</div>
          <div class="name">Marie Achour</div>
          <div class="source">Chief Product Officer, Moodle</div>
      </div>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">It&#8217;s a </span><a href="https://moodle.com/news/field-notes-where-ai-meets-learning-in-moodle-lms/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span style="font-weight: 400;">philosophy we&#8217;ve written about before</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> — and one that shapes every decision we make about AI in Moodle platforms. Agent detection is just the latest example of what that openness makes possible.</span></p>
</div>
</div>
  </div>


  <div class="acf-block flexi-block-block" id="flexi-block-block_9f9ab0e88be370ca71c0d3ff5ca14de0">
    <div class=" flexi-block">        <h2 id="block-h2-detecting-ai-agents-in-moodle-lms">Detecting AI agents in Moodle LMS</h2>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Cursive&#8217;s </span><a href="https://moodle.org/plugins/local_agentdetect" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Agent Detection Lite plugin</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> — available now in the Moodle plugins directory — is a direct example of this responsiveness in action. Built to Moodle&#8217;s standards, integrated with the Privacy API, with all data remaining local to your Moodle site, it works by expanding the session data your platform captures across five distinct detection layers: writing behaviour, site interaction patterns, browser fingerprinting, injection monitoring, and server-side request analysis. Together these capture thousands of signals per session — surfacing not just </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">what</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> was done, but </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">how</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The system is designed to be lightweight. Despite the volume of signals it collects, Cursive reports that the overall server load is less than that of a typical quiz — so detection doesn&#8217;t come at the cost of performance or learner experience.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">See Agent Detection Lite in action:</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
</div>
  <div class="text"><p></p>
</div>
  <div class="text"><p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Administrators can use it to identify where agent activity may be concentrated across their Moodle site — and make more informed decisions about assessment design, proctoring, and policy as a result.</span></p>
</div>
</div>
  </div>


  <div class="acf-block flexi-block-block" id="flexi-block-block_906d8cb2d890c1445c09aa96a27d1aac">
    <div class=" flexi-block">        <h2 id="block-h2-the-bigger-question-underneath">The bigger question underneath</h2>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Detection matters. But it&#8217;s not the end of the conversation.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">I asked Marie what agent detection in an LMS really tells us, and she reframed it in a way that stuck with me:</span></p>
</div>
  <div class="quote">
    <div class="text">When people start using tools in ways we didn&#8217;t expect, it&#8217;s easy to see that as misuse. But it&#8217;s often a signal — it tells us something about how they&#8217;re trying to engage, and where our current approaches might not be working.</div>
          <div class="name">Marie Achour</div>
          <div class="source">Chief Product Officer, Moodle</div>
      </div>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">That points to something important. If an agent can complete a task, the task itself needs a closer look. What&#8217;s often missing isn&#8217;t correctness — it&#8217;s evidence of the learning process. How someone arrived at an answer, how their thinking developed, where they revised or struggled. As Joe puts it:</span></p>
</div>
  <div class="quote">
    <div class="text">The real problem is not identifying agents. It&#8217;s validating knowledge.
</div>
          <div class="name">Joseph Thibault</div>
          <div class="source">Founder, Cursive</div>
      </div>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Moodle platforms are well-placed to support that work. Live, synchronous learning. Collaborative and portfolio-based activities. Writing tools that capture the process behind a submission, not just the final output. These are the approaches that make authentic learning visible — and make it much harder to replicate without actually doing it.</span></p>
</div>
</div>
  </div>


  <div class="acf-block flexi-block-block" id="flexi-block-block_6658ca7e73631337de79b2407558119e">
    <div class=" flexi-block">        <h2 id="block-h2-moving-forward">Moving forward</h2>
  <div class="text"><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Moments like this tend to create pressure to act quickly, and sometimes to lock things down. I understood that impulse when my worried conference attendee asked about running an LMS outside the browser entirely. But, rather than close things off, the answer to a fast-moving challenge is to have a platform that can move with you.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">For most teams, the next step isn&#8217;t to overhaul everything at once. It&#8217;s to start building a clearer picture of what&#8217;s already happening — experimenting with tools like agent detection to understand patterns, reviewing key assessments and asking what they&#8217;re really measuring, and having more open conversations with instructors and learners about where, when, and how AI is being used.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">You&#8217;re never locked into a single approach with Moodle solutions. You can test new tools, adapt your practices, and respond to what you&#8217;re seeing — without waiting for one fixed solution to arrive. And in a moment where so much feels uncertain, that ability to learn, adjust, and move forward deliberately is what makes progress possible.</span></p>
</div>
